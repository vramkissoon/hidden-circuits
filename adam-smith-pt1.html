Adam Smith is Dead - and Perhaps That’s Okay (Part 1)


My title is hyperbolic. In some ways, the recent political developments in the United States — the election of Donald Trump, the emergence of new right steeped in the tradition of liberalism, and more — indicate that the ideas most profoundly espoused in the Wealth of Nations are more alive than ever before. Such a perspective touches on a real phenomenon that I do not deny and, moreover, I myself hold.


No, this discussion is a response to yesterday’s announcement, from none other than President Donald Trump himself, that the United States will establish the External Revenue Service, the twin brother to the Internal Revenue Service, or IRS.


The External Revenue Service, which I assume will widely be referred to as the ERS, represents a major shift in the philosophy of our national economy, one that rejects a core idea of Adam Smith. The primary argument in the Weatlh of Nations aimed to persuade nations to forgo their mercantilist policies for free markets because, Smith righly claimed, that the wealth of a nation is not measured in the amount of gold or currency that it could retain but in the total value of the goods and services that the nation produced. Five cows, he reasoned, is not inherently worth more or less than the gold you get from selling five cows, and to confer more value on the currency rather than products — which the mercantile trade regimes did — would be a mistake. (That’s a really rough sketch of the argument, but you get it. If not, do your own reading. You can find his book in any major bookstore). In the course of making this argument, Smith ends up crafting one of the most beautiful and transcendental descriptions of the epiphenomenon of wealth creation through free and fair trade to have ever existed. For this reason, his work is still one of the most important pieces of economic literature ever written, and it still informs the thinking of people in the twenty-first century, more than two hundred years after it was first authored.


So, you may ask, why has a proud capitalist like Donald Trump and the free market traditionalism that underpins a large swathe of his movement estblished the External Revenue Serviee? It counters one of the core arguments of Adam Smith in favor of something much more akin to mercantilism. Is it because Adam Smith was wrong? Not exactly. Is it because of outright hypocrisy from our political machine? Not really, either (though there’s plenty of it throughout our system). If you are confused about why this shift has happened and how this movement reconciles something like the ERS with its deep belief in free enterprise, then you have not entirely reckoned with a key part of the MAGA movement and a cruicial development across the American polity as a whole, both right and left. It’s a difficult thing to articulate, and I will not try to do it all right here. I will, at least, try to articulate a semblance of an idea, as I understand it now, so that it can be further explored later by myself and maybe others


Smith was not wrong, at least for the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries and maybe even the early-twentieth centuries. Mercantalism needed to go, and the roughly free markets that emerged helped to develop much of what’s good about the world today. (Is this debatable? Absolutely. Let’s not debate it here, though. We’re going on a journey of grand narratives now, which is rougher in its treatment of truth. We can accept partial truths like this one, as long as we know they are partial and up for vigorous debate.) Much of the world has come to understand that elements of free trade are essential for a free and just society, not just for financial prosperity. Donald Trump was elected, in part, for his outspoken critique of the stifling effects that regulation on the city, state, and federal level imposed on the economic activity of the United States. Whether or not you agree with this idea — or really, to what extent, since most people paying attention to this narrative and operating in good faith can at least agree that overregulation exists — does not matter. What matters is that many Americans believe it and feel strongly about fixing it. It is crucial to the mandate of Donald Trump and this incoming administration.


So, then, why create the ERS? Why promise to impose massive tariffs on China? Why take all these positions that would make a classical liberal economist roll in his grave? I don’t pretend to know how all these policies will turn out, but I don’t think they are rooted in either stupidity or pure hypocrisy. Rather, I think that this supposed conflict in visions lays bare the heart of the economic and cultural populist movement that has fully rejected a society in which most of the wealth created, the resources controlled, and the opportunites to make a dignified living are increasingly allocated to group of people who, in the eyes of many, take more than they contribute to our society. And maybe that’s okay in a Randian moral vision, where the tautology that the capacity to obtain wealth justifies one’s possession of it. But, more and more, that’s not okay in Trump’s America. And it’s not okay in Bernie’s America. The American people smell something rotten in the foundation of our economy. And, whether or not this particular approach solves the problem, they are taking action to fix it.
